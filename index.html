Substratum

What I read in Substratum immerses me in a world inconceivable for a sensible mind, and also what I discovered changed my vision of the world, here lies what I am looking for:
On page 1.3.25.54 of this book which corresponds to the name of the library of Volume 25 page 54 on shelf 3 of wall 1 of the Hexagon.

Until I was 12, my life was still bearable, because as a child I was not yet aware that terrible torments could exist. Gradually things started to change for me, my outlook on life widened in the wrong direction, I was very drawn to books, especially Substratum. I then had a dark adolescence, with many moments of isolation, this book was going to change my life.
While reading Substratum, a chapter called "Officium tenebraurum" radically changed my view of the world, and towards the end of the book an address caught my eye, with great hesitation I made the fatal decision to go there. . Over there, one evening, along the quays, towards a narrow alley without lighting, but which was going to lead to an even darker and more mysterious place, even religious. I approached a large front door, announcing myself with a creepy old door knocker. A priest, whose hood hid his face, came to open the door for me, he seemed to be waiting for me and quickly led me to the office, where a ceremony of great magnitude had begun, an opus invaded my being, a flow of bewitching vibrations plunge me into a trance, soft and intoxicating, the scents of incense push me into the depths of an underground cathedral, and at the bottom towards the altar, candlesticks with red lights, sign, the pact of my life, for ever.

Little Nemo: Dream Another Dream.

Winsor McCay (186? – 1934) was one of the most significant American artists of the 20th century, known best for his groundbreaking newspaper comics and early animated films. Much of his most beloved work appeared in Little Nemo in Slumberland, a full-page comic that ran every Sunday from 1905 to 1926. It was on these pages that McCay expanded the world of visual narrative: his stunning sequences, expressed through vivid illustrations and inventive panel compositions, became a cornerstone of modern comics.
